5. IDEOLOGY & CURRICULUM

Core Philosophy

Hand in Hand describes its mission as "promoting and developing a shared society for Arabs and Jews in Israel" (Hebrew site) and "building inclusion and equality between Arab and Jewish citizens" (English site).

What They Teach About the Conflict

Dual-Narrative Approach:

  • Students learn the SAME historical events from BOTH perspectives
  • The curriculum explicitly acknowledges that "what represents happiness for [Jewish children] is someone else's sadness" (re: Independence Day/Nakba)
  • Dual-narrative history is taught: "learning about conflicts not just from one point of view, but from multiple ones -- to see how the same events can be shaped into different stories"

Memorial Day / Independence Day / Nakba Handling:

  • Separate ceremonies for Jewish and Arab students on Memorial Day (~30 minutes each)
  • Jewish students commemorate fallen Israeli soldiers and Israel's founding
  • Arab students discuss the Nakba -- displacement of Palestinians in 1948
  • Both groups wear white (Israeli school tradition)
  • After separation, students reunite for a concluding event designed to "transition from pain to hope"
  • Some schools release balloons as symbols of shared hope
  • The school explicitly names and acknowledges the Nakba

Key quote (Shuli Dichter, former CEO): The structure provides "room for each one to be with their own pain and not be in a position of comparing how much space is being made to each side."

Analysis: The messaging is broadly consistent, but the English site leans more into diversity/inclusion framing familiar to American donors, while the Hebrew site emphasizes civic partnership ("חברה משותפת" -- shared society). Neither site uses explicitly "post-Zionist" language. The framing is closer to "civic equality within the existing state" than to anti-Zionist ideology. However, the explicit acknowledgment of the Nakba and dual-narrative approach places them firmly outside Zionist-consensus education.

Is It Post-Zionist?

Short answer: It is not explicitly post-Zionist, but it is certainly non-Zionist in educational practice.

  • The schools operate within the Ministry of Education framework (recognized and supervised)
  • They do not reject the State of Israel or advocate for a binational state
  • However, they do treat Jewish and Arab/Palestinian narratives as equally valid
  • The dual-narrative approach implicitly rejects Zionist historiography as the sole legitimate framework
  • The 60/40 Arab-Jewish demographic split and the explicit Nakba commemoration place them well outside mainstream Israeli educational norms

A 2011 Israeli law allows the finance minister to defund schools teaching the Nakba as a day of mourning -- this law was directly relevant to Hand in Hand's practice.

7. CONTROVERSIES

A. The 2014 Arson Attack (most serious incident)

Date: November 29, 2014

Target: Max Rayne Hand in Hand School, Jerusalem (first-grade classroom)

What happened: Three Jewish extremists torched a classroom and spray-painted racist graffiti including:

  • "There is no coexistence with cancer"
  • "Death to the Arabs" (מוות לערבים)
  • "Kahane was right"

Perpetrators:

  • Yitzhak Gabai
  • Nahman Twitto
  • Shlomo Twitto
  • All linked to Lehava (anti-miscegenation organization)

Sentencing: Twitto brothers convicted via plea deal; sentenced to 2 and 2.5 years respectively; ordered to pay NIS 25,000 combined compensation.

Controversy within the controversy: Defendants were NOT charged with nationalistic/racist crimes (which could carry terrorism-level penalties in Israeli law). Mother of one arsonist publicly said she "would have done the same."

Political fallout: AJC, Israeli politicians, and international community condemned the attack. Jerusalem municipality later sued arsonists for damages. Police raided Lehava offices. The attack became a symbol of anti-coexistence extremism.

B. Jewish Student Enrollment Decline (Wadi Ara)

A major 2011 Ynet investigation titled "גשר צר מאוד: ביה"ס הדו-לשוני התרוקן מיהודים" (A Very Narrow Bridge: The Bilingual School Emptied of Jews) documented:

  • Bridge Over the Wadi school (Kafr Qara): Started with balanced enrollment
  • By ~2011: Only 23 Jewish students vs. 105 Arab students (~82% Arab)
  • Reasons cited: Security concerns in Arab sector, political tensions, commute distance
  • Parents' committee chair Hussam Abu-Bakr: "We face a real crisis... some classes have only Arab students, others just two or three Jewish students"
  • Education Ministry confirmed the trend exists "across all bilingual schools"
  • Dissenting view (Norit Maor-Shavit): Blame administrative instability, not politics

This is a structural vulnerability of the model. Arab families have high demand (waiting lists), while Jewish families face social pressure not to send children to "Arab" schools, especially in peripheral areas.

Source: https://www.ynet.co.il/articles/0,7340,L-4026730,00.html

C. Criticism from the Right

  • Lehava and affiliated groups see coexistence education as promoting "assimilation" between Jews and Arabs
  • Spray-painting, harassment of students on buses documented
  • No formal NGO Monitor report targeting Hand in Hand was found -- unusual for an organization of this profile. NGO Monitor may view it as educational rather than political-advocacy
  • Im Tirtzu has not publicly targeted Hand in Hand in the way it has targeted ACRI or NIF-funded advocacy groups
  • The 2011 Nakba Law (allowing defunding of schools marking the Nakba as mourning) was directly relevant to Hand in Hand's dual-narrative ceremonies

D. Criticism from the Left / Academic Critique

  • Haaretz op-ed (Mira Sucharov, 2016): "Are Israel's Jewish-Arab Coexistence Schools Setting Kids Up for a Cruel Fall?" -- argues graduates face a "very dissonant outside world" where coexistence skills have no real application in segregated Israeli society
  • The question of whether dual-narrative education creates false hope in a structurally unequal society is a persistent academic concern
  • Some Palestinian activists view participation in Israeli institutions (even bilingual ones) as normalization

E. October 7 War Impact

  • Families at Hand in Hand lost relatives both in Gaza and to the October 7 attacks
  • Palestinian students faced societal harassment outside school; Hand in Hand became a "rare oasis of freedom"
  • Vice Principal Engie Wattad: "For our students, this is a safe place... They feel here that they have freedom of speech"
  • Palestinian parent Morad Muna called it "the one safe place right now for their kids"
  • Despite enormous pressure, all six schools completed a full year of learning in 2024-25

9. IMPACT & ALUMNI

Quantified Outcomes

  • 700+ graduates have completed Hand in Hand schools
  • 2,000+ current students (ages 3-18)
  • 3,000+ active adult community members
  • 10,000+ indirect impact through community programming

Alumni Program

  • Supports graduates in being "civically active, advancing social change"
  • Alumni serve as "ambassadors for shared living" in universities, service institutions, and workplaces
  • No published data on specific career outcomes, military service choices, or political positions

Policy Impact

  • Municipal officials influenced to create bilingual government forms
  • Arabic translations added at local businesses in Hand in Hand communities
  • Tel Aviv-Jaffa education official: "You have changed Jaffa. There has never been such a massive demand"
  • Bilingual children's programming expanded to libraries and daycare centers beyond Hand in Hand

Academic Note

No published longitudinal outcome study tracking Hand in Hand graduates' political positions, inter-group attitudes in adulthood, or career trajectories was found. This is a significant research gap for a 25+ year old educational experiment.

12. GEFEN CONTEXT

The Gefen catalog lists Hand in Hand under program #18489 "מנהיגות חינוכית רב תרבותית" (Multicultural Educational Leadership) at 107,860 NIS across 2 cities (Haifa, Jerusalem), 5 schools, rated 2/5.

This is a tiny window into a massive organization. The 108K NIS Gefen program is likely one small Ministry-funded training initiative, while the actual organizational budget is 45M NIS (420x larger). The Gefen rating of 2/5 for this specific program should not be confused with the organization's overall quality -- which has won national and international awards including the Global Pluralism Award and Ministry of Education recognition for multiple schools.
