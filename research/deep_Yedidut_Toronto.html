Domain 4: Ideology & Programs

Core Ideology: "Social exit" -- develop programs that can be absorbed by government, then hand off. The foundation sees itself as an incubator, not a permanent service provider. Focus on reducing Israeli socioeconomic gaps, with particular emphasis on ultra-Orthodox communities and single-parent families [Source: HaMichlol]

Active Programs:

  1. El HaNefesh (אל הנפש) -- Flagship: Mental health support in ~300 Haredi educational institutions. Three pillars: (a) professional training for educators, (b) subsidized professional mental health treatment for children/youth, (c) emotional support hotline for parents and educators. 600+ practitioners. Thousands of children served. Free of charge. Listed as Gefen program #1795. Health Minister Award 2021 [Source: HaMichlol, Kol Zchut, search results]
  1. Heznekim (הזנקים): Ultra-Orthodox hi-tech integration. 10M NIS budget. Two cohorts of 50. Partnerships with HP, Check Point, Mellanox. Joint with Ministry of Labor. 101 participants evaluated at 3-year mark (67 men, 34 women) [Source: Calcalist, Pareto]
  1. Kol Achat (קול אחת): Single-parent employment support. Professional training, personal guidance, quality job placement. Has its own website: kolachat.org.il [Source: HaMichlol, Calcalist]
  1. Sviva Betucha (סביבה בטוחה): Safe school environments in Haredi schools, preventing abuse. Developed with Henrietta Szold Institute [Source: HaMichlol, Szold Institute]
  1. Alumim (עלומים): Healthy relationships and abuse prevention in religious-Zionist schools. 15,000+ students, 500+ teachers (from school year 2023-24) [Source: HaMichlol]
  1. Kidmum (קידמום): Post-crisis (COVID-19) economic and emotional support for families [Source: HaMichlol]
  1. Te'uda (תעודה): Helping at-risk Haredi girls achieve matriculation exams [Source: HaMichlol]
  1. Lapidot (לפידות): Professional development for educators working with at-risk Haredi girls [Source: HaMichlol]
  1. Orim (אורים): Parental competency program with longitudinal research [Source: HaMichlol]
  1. Merchavim (מרחבים): Prevents school dropout for at-risk Haredi girls. Achieved 1% dropout rate and 86% matriculation exam participation [Source: HaMichlol]
  1. Kol HaNa'ar (קול הנער): Identification and guidance for Haredi youth not in educational frameworks [Source: ff-yt.org]
  1. Kishurim (כישורים): Skills development [Source: ff-yt.org]

Completed Programs (achieved "social exit"):

  • Haredim in Academia (חרדים באקדמיה): 4,000+ Haredi students supported; model adopted by government
  • New Page (דף חדש): Employment model adopted by Ministry of Labor (2019)
  • Others [Source: HaMichlol]

Hebrew vs English Messaging:

  • Hebrew website (ff-yt.org): Emphasizes programs for ultra-Orthodox community specifically, uses terms like "Torah education system," works within Haredi norms
  • English website: Uses more generic social-impact language -- "non-profit incubator for effective solutions to systemic flaws," "underserved communities," does not prominently mention ultra-Orthodox focus

Domain 6: Controversies

  1. Friedberg Ecosystem and Ne'eman Foundation (MAJOR): Albert Friedberg's charitable network includes Ne'eman Foundation Canada, which had its CRA charitable status revoked in August 2024 for acting as an illegal "conduit" funneling donations to Israeli military organizations and illegal settlement groups. Friedberg donated $18M CAD to Ne'eman (2016-2022). However, Yedidut Toronto itself is NOT Ne'eman -- it is a separate entity focused on social programs. The connection is that the same donor (Friedberg) funded both [Source: Mondoweiss, Just Peace Advocates]
  1. Rabbi Druckman Funding Freeze (2019): YT froze funding for Rabbi Chaim Druckman's 'Ami' conversion programs because Druckman refused to apologize for supporting Rabbi Moti Alon (convicted of sexual harassment). YT was the second-largest donor. This shows YT was willing to take a principled stand on abuse issues [Source: Kipa]
  1. Transparency Concerns: 34% transparency rating on IsraelGives. No financial documents uploaded. No Certificate of Good Standing. For an organization handling 31M+ NIS/year, this opacity is notable [Source: IsraelGives]
  1. Friedberg Fund Performance Decline: The hedge fund source of Friedberg's philanthropy (Friedberg Mercantile Group) reportedly lost ~84% from its 2012 peak through 2021. This may explain the eventual withdrawal of funding [Source: OPM Wire]
  1. Community Reaction to Withdrawal: No public reporting found on how the Haredi community or service recipients reacted to the Friedberg withdrawal. The silence itself is notable -- 300 institutions serving thousands of children lost their primary funder, and there appears to be no public outcry or transition plan reported [Source: searches returned no results]
  1. Program Effectiveness Data: While the Heznekim program has a Pareto Group evaluation (101 participants, 3-year mark), and the Haredim in Academia program has an ROI study, the flagship El HaNefesh program has limited publicly available effectiveness data beyond participation numbers [Source: Pareto, ffi.org.il]

Part C: Analysis

Thesis: A Single-Donor Social Laboratory Facing an Existential Cliff

Yedidut Toronto is the most significant example in Israel's third sector of what happens when a single philanthropist creates an entire social services ecosystem -- then walks away. For 16 years, Albert Dov Friedberg, a Toronto hedge fund billionaire with rabbinic training, invested approximately 800 million NIS (~$270M CAD at historical rates) into programs that embedded themselves deeply within Israel's ultra-Orthodox educational and social infrastructure. The organization achieved genuine impact: 4,000+ Haredi students in academia, a mental health program reaching 300 institutions, government adoption of employment models, and a Health Minister's Award. But the entire edifice was built on a single financial pillar that has now been removed. The 2024 revenue figure (20.9M NIS, down 33% from 31M+ NIS in 2023) tells the story of an organization in contraction, with negligible government funding (under 5%) and a Shatil listing that says "no longer accepting new applications."

Pattern Naming

  1. Single-Donor Dependency Syndrome: Yedidut Toronto exemplifies a pattern where a philanthropic foundation becomes so dependent on one donor that it cannot survive the donor's withdrawal. At ~95% funding from one source, this is not a diversified organization -- it is an operating arm of the Friedberg family. The "social exit" model (handing programs to government) was the intended mitigation, but it was incomplete when the exit came.
  1. Philanthropic Laundering Structure (Friedberg Ecosystem -- NOT Yedidut Toronto itself): The Buckingham-to-Friedberg-to-multiple-entities pipeline, totaling $367M CAD in inter-foundation transfers, resembles structures that Canadian tax authorities flagged. Ne'eman Foundation's revocation for acting as a "conduit" is a red flag for the broader Friedberg ecosystem. However, it must be emphasized: Yedidut Toronto's programs (mental health for Haredi children, academic scholarships, employment training) are qualitatively different from Ne'eman's activities (military/settlement funding). They share a donor, not a mission.
  1. Cultural Brokerage Model: YT succeeded in Haredi communities not because it offered services directly, but because it embedded programs within existing Haredi institutional structures (Maayan HaChinuch HaTorani, individual Talmudei Torah). This "work through, not around" approach is why 300 institutions participated -- and why the program's continuation depends on institutional relationships, not just funding.
  1. Hebrew/English Messaging Divergence: The English-facing materials describe a generic "social incubator for underserved communities." The Hebrew materials reveal the core: a foundation focused primarily on ultra-Orthodox populations, working within Torah educational systems, with "Jewish identity" as a funding category. This is not necessarily deceptive -- it may simply reflect audience expectations -- but the gap is worth noting.

Open Questions

  1. Who is running Yedidut Toronto now? Post-October 2024, no public information exists about new leadership after Shapira and Friedberg departed. Is the organization being wound down or restructured?
  1. What happens to El HaNefesh's 300 institutions? If the primary funder is gone and government transfers cover less than 5% of operations, who is paying for subsidized mental health treatment in Haredi schools? Is the Gefen listing sufficient to sustain the program through school purchase orders?
  1. Was the "social exit" strategy achieved? YT's model was to hand programs to government. The Haredim in Academia program was reportedly completed. But El HaNefesh (the largest active program) appears to still depend on philanthropic funding. Did the Friedberg withdrawal come before the "exit" was complete?
  1. Why did Friedberg actually withdraw? The public record says the relationship "ended" in October 2024, but no explanation has been published. Possible factors: (a) Hedge fund performance decline (fund lost ~84% from 2012 peak), reducing philanthropic capacity; (b) CRA scrutiny of the broader Friedberg ecosystem following Ne'eman revocation (August 2024 -- same period); (c) Age/succession -- Friedberg is elderly; (d) Mission completion (unlikely, given active programs).
  1. Is there a connection between Ne'eman revocation timing and Yedidut withdrawal timing? Ne'eman's charitable status was revoked in August 2024. Friedberg's relationship with Yedidut Toronto ended October 2024. The temporal proximity is striking. Did CRA enforcement activity cause Friedberg to restructure or reduce his Canadian charitable activities?
  1. What is the 2024 revenue breakdown? The 20.9M NIS figure from obudget -- how much is reserves being spent down vs. new income? Is the organization burning through endowment?
  1. Program effectiveness for El HaNefesh: Beyond participation numbers (300 institutions, 600 practitioners, "thousands of children"), what are the clinical outcomes? How does subsidized therapy in Haredi schools compare to standard mental health services? The Health Minister Award suggests recognition, but published outcome data is missing.
  1. Why only 9 employees? For an organization with 31M NIS revenue and programs in 300+ institutions, 9 employees suggests heavy outsourcing or subcontracting. Where does the money actually go?
