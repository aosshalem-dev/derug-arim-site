Founding History

ELI was founded in 1979 by Dr. Hanita Zimrin (born Hanita Goldschmid, March 17, 1945 – May 4, 2021). Zimrin was a Jerusalem native who became the first person in Israel to receive a PhD in Social Work (1978). She served as a professor at Tel Aviv University's School of Social Work for 20 years (1970–1988), heading the Children and Youth Unit.

In 1979, she established ELI as the first voluntary organization in Israel dedicated specifically to child abuse, at a time when the subject was not publicly acknowledged in Israeli society. She is credited with putting child abuse on the national agenda.

Important note: The user asked about a 1948 founding. This is incorrect. ELI was founded in 1979, not 1948. The confusion may stem from Israel's founding year. ELI is 45+ years old, not 75+.

Key Insight: ELI is overwhelmingly donation-funded. Government funding is negligible — 5,000 NIS over three years against a ~7.6M annual turnover. The Ministry of Justice contracts are for specific expert services (psychological evaluations, sexual risk assessments, court-ordered consultations).

However, ELI also receives indirect government funding through the Ministry of Social Services and National Insurance Institute (Bituach Leumi), which fund treatments provided through local social service bureaus. These payments go through the municipalities, not directly to ELI, and therefore may not appear on obudget.org as direct transfers.

Founder Biography

Dr. Hanita Zimrin (1945–2021):

  • Born Hanita Goldschmid in Jerusalem, grew up in Beit HaKerem
  • First Israeli PhD in Social Work (1978)
  • Professor at Tel Aviv University School of Social Work (1970–1988)
  • Founded ELI in 1979
  • Received ISPCAN Distinguished Career Award (1996) from the International Society for Prevention of Child Abuse and Neglect
  • Member of the editorial board of The International Journal of Child Abuse and Neglect (US)
  • Published extensively on child abuse in Israel
  • Died May 4, 2021, age 76, after illness. Resided in Herzliya.

4. Programs

4.1 Clinical Treatment Center

  • Main center: Rishon LeZion (serves surrounding municipalities)
  • New center (2023): Ra'anana
  • Branches in multiple cities
  • 20 new therapy rooms opened post-October 7 to handle war-related trauma
  • 12,000 therapy hours annually
  • 80% success rate (clients returning to normal functioning)
  • Treatment modalities: individual, group, dyadic, family therapy
  • Populations served:
  • Children/adolescents who experienced abuse
  • Adults abused in childhood
  • Parents who perpetrate abuse (treatment for abusers)
  • Minors with inappropriate/harmful sexual behavior
  • Children with disabilities
  • Arabic-speaking populations
  • Russian-speaking populations
  • Ultra-Orthodox communities (closed communities)
  • Family crises (adolescence, depression, loss, divorce)

4.2 School-Based Prevention (ELI on Stage)

  • ~100,000 children annually through theatrical education
  • ~100 schools, ~300 activities per year
  • Disclosure rate: 1.5% of children participating either disclose abuse or are identified as at risk
  • Operating in schools since 1978/1979

Performance titles by age:

  • Kindergarten: "Yael Learns to Protect Her Body" (יעל לומדת לשמור על הגוף שלה) — body safety
  • Primary school: "Bad Film" (סרט רע), "Mystery on the Web" (סוד ברשת) — cyberbullying, online safety
  • Middle/High school: "Something of You in Me" (משהו ממך בי) — abuse awareness

Content focus: Body boundaries, physical and sexual abuse prevention, cyberbullying, online safety, social exclusion. Performances adapted for religious-national and ultra-Orthodox sectors.

Post-performance protocol: Social workers and counselors lead discussions after each show to identify at-risk children.

KEY FINDING — SEL and Gender Content:

  • ELI's school programs are NOT SEL (Social-Emotional Learning) frameworks. They are narrowly focused on abuse prevention and detection.
  • The organization does address "healthy sexuality" (מיניות בריאה) in its teacher training, but this refers to age-appropriate sexual development awareness for educators — not gender ideology or identity programming.
  • The training covers: natural developmental stages, privacy boundaries, social media behavior, recognizing abuse indicators, mandatory reporting.
  • No evidence found of gender identity content, pronoun education, LGBTQ+ programming, or values-based social engineering in ELI's school programs.
  • However, the US GuideStar profile lists alignment with SDG 5 "Gender Equality" — this appears to be a standard Candid/GuideStar classification rather than a programmatic focus.

4.3 Training Center

  • Recognized by the Ministry of Social Services for therapist training
  • Professional development for therapists, welfare workers, medical professionals, legal professionals, educators
  • Lectures, seminars, customized training programs
  • Two training tracks for educators:
  1. Healthy sexuality (age-appropriate development, boundaries)
  2. Protection and sexual abuse prevention (identification, response, mandatory reporting)

5. Ideological Evolution

Original Mission (1979)

Clinical child abuse prevention, treatment, and professional training. No political or ideological agenda.

Current Mission (2025)

Clinical child abuse prevention, treatment, professional training, school-based prevention, IDF partnership. No political or ideological agenda.

Assessment: Has the Mission Shifted?

No significant ideological drift detected. ELI has expanded its scope (adding school programs, IDF partnership, mobile therapy, cyberbullying) but has not shifted its core mission. Key observations:

  1. No political advocacy. Unlike ACRI or the National Council for the Child (המועצה הלאומית לשלום הילד), ELI does not engage in policy lobbying beyond child protection issues.
  2. No values-based programming. ELI's school programs teach body safety and abuse detection, not social values or identity frameworks.
  3. Sector-neutral. ELI adapts programs for secular, religious-national, and ultra-Orthodox populations. This indicates pragmatic service delivery, not ideological targeting.
  4. Clinical focus maintained. The organization's primary identity remains as a clinical treatment provider, not an advocacy organization.
  5. No Hebrew/English messaging gap. Both language versions describe the same mission: child protection from abuse. No semantic displacement detected.

The Blaustein Foundation grant being classified under "Strengthening Israeli Democracy" is a funder classification, not ELI's self-identification. ELI does not position itself as a democracy organization.

Summary

ELI is a small, family-run, clinically focused child protection organization. It does what it says it does: treat abuse victims, train professionals, and run prevention programs in schools. There is no evidence of ideological agenda, political activism, mission drift, or controversial content in its programs. Its governance is weak (family board, no independent oversight) but its operational integrity appears strong (Platinum transparency seal, 96.56% program ratio, ISPCAN recognition, IDF trust). Among the organizations researched in this series, ELI is notable for its absence of red flags.
